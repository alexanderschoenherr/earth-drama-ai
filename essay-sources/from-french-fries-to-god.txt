From French Fries to God

Love is not one flat feeling. It climbs from appetite to beauty, from care to justice, from private emotion to political power, from separation to God.

We use the same word for french fries and God.

I love french fries.

I love that song.

I love my car.

I love you.

Love your neighbor.

God is love.

Either love is one of the sloppiest words in the English language, or it is one of the deepest. Either it has been stretched until it means almost nothing, or it is one of the only words large enough to hold almost everything.

Maybe both.

We use love for snacks, songs, lovers, children, strangers, enemies, country, justice, beauty, sex, memory, prayer, and God. We throw it across a dinner table. We text it casually. We say it at weddings. We whisper it at deathbeds. We use it when we are flirting, joking, grieving, worshiping, or ordering food.

Somehow the word survives all of it.

That should tell us something.

Maybe love is not one flat feeling.

Maybe love is a ladder.

At the bottom, love means pleasure.

I love french fries.

The body encounters the world and says yes. Salt, heat, fat, texture, hunger, satisfaction. This is not divine love. This is not moral love. This is not the love that would lay down its life for another. But it is still a form of relation. Something outside the self touches the self and produces delight.

A small yes.

A bodily yes.

A yes at the level of appetite.

Then love rises.

I love that smell.

Now love is not only hunger. It is memory, atmosphere, mood, recognition. A smell can bring back a house, a season, a person, a childhood, a room that no longer exists. The world enters through the senses and rearranges the inner life.

Then love becomes beauty.

I love this song.

A song does not merely please the body. It opens something. It touches grief, longing, joy, memory, hope. It can make a person feel less alone. It can reveal an inner world the person had not yet named.

Then love becomes attachment.

I love my home.

I love my city.

I love my car.

These are not just objects or places. They become extensions of identity. A car can mean freedom. A city can mean destiny. A home can mean safety. A room can carry the memory of who we were becoming when we lived there. The object is not alive in the way a person is alive, but it holds a piece of our story.

Then love becomes affection.

I love my friend.

Now love is no longer appetite, taste, preference, beauty, or possession. It is recognition. Friendship says: I see you. I choose you. Your life matters to me. You are not mine by blood or law or necessity, but somehow you have become part of the shape of my life.

Then love becomes desire.

I am in love with you.

Now the word becomes dangerous. Eros enters. Longing enters. Projection enters. The hunger for union enters. Romantic love can reveal beauty, but it can also invent it. It can open the soul or trap it inside fantasy. It can become reverence, or it can become possession wearing perfume.

Desire becomes love only when it becomes responsible for the life it touches.

Then love becomes protection.

I love my child.

Now the word has climbed.

Love is no longer mainly about what something gives me. It becomes what another life requires of me. A child does not exist to gratify the parent. A child calls the parent out of the self and into responsibility. Love becomes vigilance, sacrifice, shelter, nourishment, patience, terror, hope, and the willingness to be interrupted by another person’s need to live.

The higher love rises, the less it asks:

What do I get?

The more it asks:

What must I protect?

Then love becomes moral.

Love your neighbor.

Now the word expands beyond preference. Your neighbor does not have to be attractive to you. Your neighbor does not have to entertain you. Your neighbor does not have to belong to your family, your tribe, your party, your religion, your class, your race, your story.

The command is not based on taste.

It is based on dignity.

At this level, love says: you do not have to belong to me for your life to matter.

Then love becomes justice.

Because if your neighbor matters, politics cannot be morally neutral.

If children matter, child poverty is not a policy inconvenience. It is a spiritual failure made visible.

If bodies matter, healthcare is not merely an industry. It is a test of whether a society protects life.

If workers matter, wages are not merely numbers. They are statements about whose time is sacred and whose exhaustion is acceptable.

If the vulnerable matter, the budget is not merely math. It is a moral document.

Love that never touches power remains private sentiment.

Love that enters power becomes justice.

Then love becomes perception.

This may be the level we misunderstand most.

Love is not only what you feel.

Love is how you see.

Hatred shrinks reality. Fear edits reality. Ego defends reality against anything that threatens its position. Tribe turns reality into a weapon. A wound can become a lens so narrow that the whole world must pass through the injury before it is allowed to be seen.

Love refuses that collapse.

Love keeps perception alive.

Love is the discipline of seeing reality whole.

That does not mean refusing judgment. It does not mean pretending cruelty is harmless. It does not mean calling evil good. It means refusing to let hatred reduce the world to the size of your wound.

Love sees more than fear can tolerate.

Then love becomes unity.

Not “I like everything.”

Not “everything is fine.”

Not “nothing should be confronted.”

Unity is not anesthesia.

Unity means the boundary between self and world is no longer experienced as absolute. The other is not merely an object outside the self. The stranger is not merely a stranger. The enemy is not merely an enemy. The child across town is not someone else’s problem. The suffering body is not outside the body of the whole.

Love is what happens when the part remembers the whole.

Love is what happens when a cell remembers it belongs to the body.

And then, at the top, love becomes God.

Not because every use of the word is equally divine. Loving french fries is not the same as loving God. That distinction matters.

The mistake is not using one word for all of it.

The mistake is pretending every use of the word happens at the same depth.

Love begins as attraction, matures into care, deepens into responsibility, and culminates as unity.

The word climbs because consciousness climbs.

The meaning of love depends on the consciousness speaking it.

An appetite-self says, I love what gratifies me.

A possessive self says, I love what I can control.

A wounded self says, I love what confirms my wound.

A tribal self says, I love what belongs to my side.

A moral self says, I love what deserves dignity.

An awakened self says, I love because nothing is finally separate from me.

The ladder is not only in the word.

The ladder is inside the one who says it.

This is why Marianne Williamson has been so easy to misunderstand.

When Marianne says love, many people hear the lower rungs.

Softness.

Niceness.

Sentiment.

Private feeling.

A greeting card trying to run for office.

They hear someone bringing a heart word into a power culture, and they assume the heart word must be weak because the culture has taught them that power is serious and love is decorative.

But Marianne is not using love that way.

She is not talking about sweetness. She is not talking about avoiding conflict. She is not talking about pretending cruelty does not exist. She is not asking America to hold hands and feel better while the machinery keeps grinding people down.

She is speaking from the upper rungs.

Love as moral force.

Love as spiritual intelligence.

Love as public responsibility.

Love as correct perception.

Love as the power that protects life.

Love as the force that tells politics what it is for.

That is not sentimentality.

That is a theory of power.

America hears a heart word through a power culture. That is the problem.

A power culture understands domination more quickly than care. It understands force, money, polling, status, punishment, leverage, spectacle, and victory. It knows how to respect aggression when aggression calls itself realism.

But when love enters the room, the culture does not know where to put it.

So it files love under feeling.

Spirit under private belief.

Morality under personal preference.

Care under charity.

Consciousness under self-help.

The soul outside the machinery of public life.

Then when Marianne speaks of love in politics, people think she is trying to make politics softer.

She is actually trying to make politics answer to something higher.

That is the difference.

Love is not the opposite of power.

Love is the vertical force that tells horizontal power what it is for.

The horizontal plane is the world of money, status, policy, institutions, elections, markets, laws, bodies, borders, force, and survival. It matters. People need food, housing, healthcare, safety, wages, infrastructure, legal protection. Love that refuses the horizontal remains a feeling in the clouds.

But the vertical plane is the world of meaning, conscience, God, truth, dignity, moral law, spiritual depth, and the soul.

Without the horizontal, love never becomes real.

Without the vertical, power forgets why it exists.

This is the wound Marianne keeps pointing toward.

America did not merely separate church from state. That separation, properly understood, protects us from theocracy and religious domination. But beneath that legal principle, the culture went further.

It separated spirit from power.

Morality from policy.

Care from economics.

Inner life from national life.

Consciousness from institutions.

Then it acted shocked when its institutions became unconscious.

A civilization that separates love from power should not be surprised when its power becomes cruel.

This is why Marianne’s language sounds strange in the political arena. She is speaking vertically inside a horizontal culture. She is using a word that climbs inside a culture trained to flatten it.

She says love.

The culture hears feelings.

She means the force that asks what a budget does to a child.

She says love.

The culture hears softness.

She means the power that refuses to dehumanize even while confronting what is cruel.

She says love.

The culture hears vibes.

She means the discipline of seeing reality whole when fear wants to reduce it.

She says love.

The culture hears weakness.

She means the highest form of strength: power governed by conscience.

To a consciousness organized by fear, love sounds unserious.

To a consciousness organized by unity, fear sounds primitive.

That is the gap.

The question is not whether love belongs in politics.

Politics is already organized by invisible spiritual forces.

Fear belongs.

Greed belongs.

Resentment belongs.

Domination belongs.

Narcissism belongs.

Tribal hatred belongs.

The question is not whether consciousness shapes power.

It already does.

The only question is which consciousness.

A politics without love is not neutral.

It is merely governed by something lower.

That is why love has to be translated.

It cannot remain a beautiful word floating above the wreckage.

Love must show what it does.

Love protects.

Love builds.

Love funds.

Love confronts.

Love tells the truth.

Love disciplines power.

Love repairs what cruelty breaks.

Love refuses to make peace with systems that devour human beings and call themselves realistic.

Do not abandon love because the culture hears it badly.

Arm it.

Not with cruelty. Not with domination. Not by making love mimic the forces it is meant to transform.

Arm love with clarity.

Arm it with policy.

Arm it with moral judgment.

Arm it with institutional consequence.

Arm it with the power to say no.

A politics of love is not a politics without conflict.

It is a politics that knows what conflict is for.

Not fighting to humiliate.

Not fighting to dominate.

Not fighting to feed the wound.

Fighting to protect life from what degrades it.

That is why love has to climb.

At the bottom, love says: this pleases me.

Higher, it says: this moves me.

Higher, it says: this matters to me.

Higher, it says: I am responsible to this.

Higher, it says: I must protect this.

Higher, it says: this is not separate from me.

At the highest level, love is no longer merely a feeling moving from one private person toward another private person.

It becomes the intelligence of the whole moving through the part.

It becomes the force by which power remembers life.

That is what America missed.

Marianne Williamson was not asking America to make politics softer.

She was asking America to become conscious enough to understand what power is for.

Love starts as pleasure.

It rises into beauty.

It deepens into care.

It becomes responsibility.

It becomes justice.

It becomes perception.

It becomes unity.

It becomes God.

America heard a feeling.

She meant an awakening.
