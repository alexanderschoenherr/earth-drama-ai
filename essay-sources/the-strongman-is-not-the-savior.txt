The Strongman Is Not the Savior

How the attacks on James Talarico reveal a Christianity trained to recognize domination faster than mercy.

“The opposite of faith is not doubt. The opposite of faith is control.”

That is James Talarico’s diagnosis.

Not only of Christian nationalism.

Of what happens when religion forgets love and reaches for power instead.

Talarico is 37 years old: a Texas Democrat, former middle school teacher, Presbyterian seminarian, state representative, and now the Democratic nominee for U.S. Senate in Texas.

He talks about politics as if it is not only a war for control, but a test of conscience.

“Politics is just another word for how we treat our neighbors.”

That line sounds gentle until you remember who Jesus told people to count as their neighbor.

The poor.
The stranger.
The sick.
The prisoner.
The enemy.
The person power teaches us not to see.

For Talarico, faith is not supposed to stay sealed inside the sanctuary. It has to enter the daily machinery of how human beings are treated.

That is why his critique of Christian nationalism is so direct.

Christian nationalism, as he describes it, is not Christianity.

It is the worship of power in Christ’s name.

Political power.
Social power.
Economic power.

Control dressed up as faith.

And if that sounds like an attack on Christianity, it is worth asking which Christianity is being defended.

Because Jesus did not tell his followers to seize the state, humiliate the stranger, worship the ruler, or confuse cruelty with truth.

He blessed the poor.

He touched the sick.

He defended the accused.

He warned the rich.

He exposed religious hypocrisy.

He told people to love their enemies.

So when Talarico says the opposite of faith is control, he is not inventing a new Christianity.

He is pointing back to the old one.

And for that, Donald Trump said he was “insulting to Jesus Christ.”

A 37-year-old Christian seminarian warned against turning faith into domination, and the most powerful man in the country answered by making him the blasphemy.

Trump did not argue with Talarico’s theology.

He turned him into a caricature.

Alfred E. Neuman.
Weak on crime.
Open borders.

The point was not persuasion.

It was humiliation.

Make him ridiculous.
Make him soft.
Make him fake.
Make him un-Texan.
Make him un-Christian.

Then came the attack on his manhood:

“Low-T Talarico.”

The insult is stupid.

The worldview underneath it is not.

It says power means domination.
It says manhood means threat.
It says strength means the ability to humiliate.
It says mercy is suspicious.
It says love is weakness unless it comes armed.

Then the religious attacks said the same thing in church language.

On a Christian nationalist podcast, Talarico was treated not as a brother, neighbor, or political opponent, but as a spiritual threat.

He was called a demon.

He was described as an enemy.

One host said he prayed that God would kill him.

Another pastor spoke of wanting him crucified with Christ.

And Talarico answered with the kind of sentence Christianity claims to revere but power never knows what to do with:

“You may pray for my death, Pastor, but I still love you.”

There it was.

The whole conflict in miniature.

A Christian man warned against the worship of power, and the worshipers of power called him weak.

A Christian man was placed in the language of death, and he reached for love.

A Christian man was cursed, and he refused to curse back.

That should have clarified everything.

Instead, it revealed the crisis.

Not that Christians disagree about politics. Christians have always disagreed about politics.

The crisis is that a movement trained by domination can lose the ability to recognize mercy as power.

It can recognize the Bible in a ruler’s hand.

It can recognize the cross as a brand.

It can recognize cruelty when cruelty is renamed toughness.

It can recognize humiliation when humiliation is called masculinity.

But when someone answers hatred with love, speaks of the poor and the stranger, and refuses to turn faith into control, it treats him like a threat.

That is not new.

That is the old story.

Religion and power meet a voice shaped by mercy.

And they reach for the cross.

The Power They Recognize

The problem is not that this movement is too Christian.

The problem is that it has learned to call domination Christianity.

That is not ordinary hypocrisy.

Hypocrisy is when people fail to live up to their values.

This is worse.

This is when the values themselves have been replaced.

Domination becomes strength.
Cruelty becomes truth.
Humiliation becomes masculinity.
Control becomes faith.
And love becomes weakness.

But domination is not power in the Christian sense.

Domination is fear organized as control.

It controls because it is afraid. It humiliates because it is insecure. It punishes because it cannot persuade. It conquers because it does not know how to belong.

It calls itself order, but it leaves death behind.

There is a kind of force that protects life. That distinction matters. If someone is harming a child, you stop them. If a tyrant is crushing the vulnerable, you resist. If cruelty is organized into law, love does not sit quietly and call passivity holiness.

But protective force is not domination.

Protective force says: life must be protected.

Domination says: I must rule.

That is the difference.

One serves life.

The other feeds on it.

Domination is power over life.

Love is power in service of life.

And love, rightly understood, is not soft.

It is not sentimental.

It is not the politics of being nice to people who are destroying the world.

Love is the disciplined power to protect and serve life.

It can confront. It can resist. It can tell the truth. It can set a boundary. It can stand between the vulnerable and the machine.

But it does not need humiliation to prove it is real.

It does not need cruelty to prove it is strong.

It does not need an enemy to know itself.

That is the power Jesus taught.

Not domination.

Not control.

Not the strongman fantasy.

Life-protecting power.

That is why Talarico’s response mattered.

“You may pray for my death, Pastor, but I still love you.”

That sentence did not make him weak.

It made the conflict visible.

Because if your Christianity cannot recognize power in that sentence, what kind of power has it been trained to recognize?

If it can see strength in Trump’s mockery but weakness in Talarico’s mercy, what has it mistaken for Christ?

That is the question.

Not whether Talarico is perfect.

No politician is.

Not whether every Christian must agree with his politics.

They do not.

The question is simpler and more dangerous:

What kind of power does American Christianity recognize as holy?

Because the Gospel does not worship the strongman.

It exposes him.

The Manhood They Mistook for Power

“Low-T Talarico” is a childish insult.

But childish insults often reveal adult beliefs.

That phrase is not really about testosterone.

It is about power.

It is a way of saying that Talarico’s kind of Christianity is not manly enough because it does not perform domination.

It does not threaten, punish, humiliate, swagger, or turn cruelty into proof of seriousness.

That is the wound underneath the insult.

American culture has been trained to recognize domination as masculine.

It calls domination strong.
Realistic.
Leadership.
Backbone.
Courage.

Then it looks at another kind of power — patient, relational, merciful, protective, life-giving — and calls it soft.

Feminine.
Weak.
Unserious.

But that second kind of power is much closer to the power Jesus taught.

This is not biology. Women can dominate. Men can nurture. These are not biological prisons. They are ways of relating to power.

The problem is that our culture has gendered power incorrectly. It has taught people to see domination as manhood and life-protecting power as weakness.

That is why Trump looks strong to so many people.

That is the trick hidden inside the word “strongman.”

To people trained by domination, even the warning sounds like praise.

Strongman.

They hear the first half and miss the danger in the whole word.

They hear strong.

They hear man.

They hear what they have been taught to want.

A man who dominates.
A man who does not apologize.
A man who makes enemies afraid.
A man who bends the room toward himself.
A man who turns cruelty into proof that he cannot be controlled.

But a strongman is not simply a strong man.

A strongman is strength corrupted into rule.

Strength without humility.

Strength without mercy.

Strength without service.

Strength that has stopped protecting life and started worshiping itself.

True strength does not need to humiliate the weak.

True strength does not need to perform cruelty.

True strength does not need an enemy to know it exists.

True strength can protect without dominating.

It can confront without dehumanizing.

It can stand between the vulnerable and the machine.

It can absorb hatred without becoming hatred.

It can love without surrendering truth.

That is the difference between the strongman and the strong man.

One needs control.

The other has character.

One performs power.

The other serves life.

And by that standard, Jesus was not weak.

He was the opposite of the strongman.

He was the strong man domination could not understand.

Jesus did not need to humiliate the weak to prove he was strong.

He did not need to dominate the vulnerable to prove he was powerful.

He did not need cruelty to look masculine.

Jesus wept.

Jesus washed feet.

Jesus touched the sick.

Jesus defended the accused.

Jesus fed the hungry.

Jesus confronted religious hypocrisy.

Jesus forgave while being executed.

That was not weakness.

That was power without domination.

That was love with a spine.

So maybe the crisis of masculinity is not that men have become too soft.

Maybe the crisis is that too many men have been taught to confuse domination with purpose.

We have had centuries of men leading through conquest, empire, control, extraction, and force. We have dominated land, bodies, labor, politics, nature, and one another, and called it civilization.

But domination has not healed us.

It has not made us whole.

It has not made us safe.

It has not taught us how to love.

So no, we are not done with men.

We are done with domination masquerading as manhood.

And Christianity, if it remembered its own savior, should know the difference.

Trump Is the Government Now

Trump is not only a man performing domination.

He is not only a television strongman.

He is not only a politician turning cruelty into spectacle.

He is the president of the United States.

The executive face of the American state.

That changes the moral weight of everything.

When Trump mocks Talarico, it is not just one man insulting another.

When Trump invokes Jesus, it is not just one man making a religious claim.

When Trump teaches millions of people to see mercy as weakness and domination as strength, he is not only shaping a campaign. He is shaping the moral imagination of the country from the highest office in the land.

The presidency is not just a microphone.

It is machinery.

It is borders.

It is prisons.

It is police power.

It is military power.

It is surveillance.

It is deportation.

It is prosecution.

It is pardon.

It is the authority to decide which suffering becomes visible and which suffering can be explained away.

But machinery does not rule by force alone.

It also rules through story.

Every nation tells a story about itself. America’s story has always carried a religious charge: flags, sacrifice, destiny, military honor, national innocence, and the belief that God is somehow uniquely invested in America’s victory.

At its best, that public faith can remind a nation that it is accountable to something higher than itself.

At its worst, it becomes national ego dressed as divinity.

Then God gets attached to the flag.
God gets attached to the military.
God gets attached to wealth.
God gets attached to national innocence.
God gets attached to “our side.”
God gets attached to victory.

That is not faith judging power from above.

That is power stealing the language of faith to bless itself.

True faith says: America must be judged by love, justice, truth, and God.

False civil religion says: America is already God’s instrument, so American power is righteous.

That is how spirit gets corrupted by power.

And once that corruption happens, many intelligent people become allergic to spiritual language in politics altogether.

Understandably.

But incompletely.

Because the answer to false religion in politics is not a politics with no soul.

It is a politics whose spiritual language judges power instead of flattering it.

That is what makes the difference.

God-language can humble a nation.

Or it can intoxicate one.

It can call power to account.

Or it can bless power in advance.

It can stand above the state as judgment.

Or it can become the state’s perfume.

So when Trump wraps domination in religious language, Christians have to ask what kind of “God” is being invoked.

The God who judges nations?

Or the god who flatters them?

The God of the stranger, the prisoner, the poor, and the crucified?

Or the god of national ego, punishment, victory, and control?

Because Christianity is not only concerned with private belief.

It is concerned with what power does to bodies.

The body of the stranger.
The body of the poor.
The body of the prisoner.
The body of the sick.
The body of the protester.
The body of the politically inconvenient.

The body made disposable by law.

The body made threatening by propaganda.

The body made invisible by policy.

So when Trump invokes God while using state power against the stranger, the poor, the prisoner, the immigrant, the protester, and the politically inconvenient, Christians have to ask what story they are standing inside.

Are they following Christ?

Or are they applauding the machinery that crucified him?

That question is not rhetorical decoration.

It is the center of the matter.

Because Christianity should never forget what happened the last time religion and empire agreed that a merciful man was dangerous.

The Cross Was State Power

Before the cross was a necklace, it was a warning.

Before it was carved into church walls, lifted above altars, printed on Bibles, or worn around the necks of people who have forgotten its original violence, it was a machine of public terror.

Rome did not invent the cross to inspire private devotion.

Rome used the cross to make bodies speak.

A crucified body said:

This is what happens when you threaten the order of things.

This is what happens when empire decides you are dangerous.

This is what happens when power wants everyone watching to understand the cost of disobedience.

That is what Christians have to remember before they turn the cross into an ornament of belonging.

Jesus was not killed by misunderstanding alone.

He was not killed because people were generally mean.

He was not killed because the world had bad energy.

He was killed by state power.

He was arrested, judged, mocked, beaten, displayed, and executed through the machinery of empire.

The crucifixion was legal.

That did not make it righteous.

That is one of the most terrifying truths in the Gospel: evil does not always arrive as chaos. Sometimes it arrives as order. Sometimes it arrives through procedure. Sometimes it arrives with paperwork, soldiers, religious justification, and the calm voice of someone saying this is necessary.

Rome knew how to do that.

Rome knew how to call domination peace.

Rome knew how to call violence order.

Rome knew how to make the suffering of one body into a lesson for everyone else.

And Jesus became dangerous to that order.

Not because he was powerful in the way Rome understood power.

He did not command an army.

He did not hold office.

He did not control a court.

He did not have soldiers, prisons, borders, or weapons at his disposal.

And still he was treated as a threat.

Because mercy can threaten an empire more deeply than a sword.

A sword challenges the empire on the empire’s own terms.

Mercy challenges the terms themselves.

Jesus blessed the poor in a world built on hierarchy.

He touched the unclean in a world built on separation.

He forgave sinners in a world built on punishment.

He defended the accused in a world built on accusation.

He warned the rich in a world that treated wealth as proof of favor.

He exposed religious hypocrisy in a world where religion could be used to protect power.

He announced a kingdom that did not need Caesar’s permission to exist.

That was the threat.

Not weakness.

Not softness.

Not naïveté.

A different authority.

A different order.

A different definition of power.

That is why religion and empire both appear in the story of the cross.

Rome supplied the machinery.

Religious authority helped supply the accusation.

The state made the execution possible.

Religion helped make it seem righteous.

That is the pattern.

Power sees mercy and calls it disorder.

Power sees forgiveness and calls it weakness.

Power sees solidarity with the poor and calls it radicalism.

Power sees love of enemy and calls it betrayal.

Power sees a person it cannot control and calls him dangerous.

Then religion comes along and gives the danger a holy name.

Demon.
Blasphemer.
Enemy of God.
Insulting to Jesus Christ.

That is why the Talarico story matters.

Not because James Talarico is Jesus. No candidate should be turned into a savior.

The point is that the reaction to him reveals how the old pattern still works.

A Christian man warns against the worship of power.

He speaks about neighbors, mercy, poverty, and the danger of confusing faith with control.

He answers hatred with love.

And what happens?

He is mocked as weak.

He is mocked as unmanly.

He is called spiritually dangerous.

He is called a demon.

He is placed in the language of death.

That does not make him Christ.

It makes him a test.

Because Christianity tells people to judge by fruits.

So look at the fruits.

One kind of power humiliates.

Another kind forgives.

One kind threatens.

Another kind refuses to hate back.

One kind turns faith into control.

Another kind warns that control is the opposite of faith.

One kind reaches for the state.

Another kind points back to the neighbor.

And the movement that claims Jesus has to decide which power it recognizes.

That is the question hiding inside the cross.

Would Christians have recognized Jesus before Rome killed him?

Or would they have agreed that he was dangerous?

Would they have seen the Son of God?

Or would they have seen a threat to order?

Would they have heard good news?

Or would they have heard blasphemy?

Would they have followed the merciful man?

Or would they have trusted the machinery that crucified him?

This is not ancient history.

It is the recurring temptation of religion whenever it gets close to power.

To trade mercy for order.

To trade love for control.

To trade truth for belonging.

To trade the crucified Christ for the empire that crucified him.

That is why Christians should be careful when a ruler flatters their faith.

Careful when the state borrows their language.

Careful when domination arrives with Bible verses.

Careful when cruelty is renamed strength.

Careful when the machinery of punishment claims to defend God.

Because the cross was not only a sign of what Christ suffered.

It was an exposure of the powers that made him suffer.

And the warning is still there:

Do not worship the machinery that crucified him.

The Stranger Is Still Christ

Jesus told Christians where to find him.

Not in the palace.

Not in the ruler’s hand.

Not in the spectacle of national greatness.

Not in the machinery of domination.

He said:

I was hungry.
I was thirsty.
I was a stranger.
I was naked.
I was sick.
I was in prison.

And then he made the identification impossible to soften:

Whatever you did to the least of these, you did to me.

That is not a vague command to be nice.

It is a recognition test.

Jesus does not merely tell his followers to care about vulnerable people. He identifies himself with them.

The hungry are not a charity category.

The stranger is not a political inconvenience.

The prisoner is not a disposable body.

The sick are not a burden.

The poor are not failed citizens.

They are the places where Christ said he would be found.

So if Jesus identifies himself with the stranger, the way a government treats immigrants is not spiritually neutral.

If Jesus identifies himself with the prisoner, the way a movement talks about punishment is not spiritually neutral.

If Jesus identifies himself with the poor, the way a politics treats poverty is not spiritually neutral.

If Jesus identifies himself with the sick, the way a society treats suffering is not spiritually neutral.

Christianity does not allow its followers to worship Christ in heaven while despising him in the bodies of the vulnerable.

That is the problem with a politics that invokes Jesus while training people to fear the stranger, blame the poor, discard the prisoner, and treat mercy as weakness.

It is not simply being harsh.

It is missing Christ where Christ said he would be.

This is why Talarico becomes so threatening to Christian nationalism.

He keeps pointing back to the people Jesus pointed toward.

The poor.
The stranger.
The sick.
The vulnerable.
The prisoner.
The neighbor.
The enemy who must still be loved.
The God who cannot be reduced to a flag, a party, or a ruler.

That kind of Christianity threatens any movement that wants Jesus as a symbol but not as a command.

It threatens a politics that wants the cross as identity but not as judgment.

It threatens a faith that wants Christ’s name without Christ’s demands.

Because Matthew 25 does not ask whether a ruler said “God.”

It does not ask whether a nation printed prayers on its money.

It does not ask whether a politician held a Bible.

It asks what happened to the hungry.

What happened to the stranger.

What happened to the sick.

What happened to the prisoner.

And that is the test American Christianity keeps trying to escape.

It wants to debate whether Trump is useful.

It wants to debate whether Talarico is too progressive.

It wants to debate whether Christian nationalism is just patriotism with hymns.

But Jesus makes the question simpler and more dangerous.

Did you recognize me?

When I was hungry, did you feed me?

When I was a stranger, did you welcome me?

When I was sick, did you care for me?

When I was in prison, did you come to me?

Or did you call me illegal?

Did you call me weak?

Did you call me dangerous?

Did you call me a criminal?

Did you call me fake?

Did you call me demonic?

That is the judgment hidden inside the Gospel.

Christ does not only arrive where religion expects him.

He arrives where power tells us not to look.

He arrives in the people empire teaches us to fear.

And when a movement cannot recognize him there, it should be careful claiming his name.

Would They Recognize Him?

That is the question underneath all of this.

If Jesus appeared in American public life now, would the people who claim him recognize him?

Not the Jesus already approved by culture.

Not the stained-glass Jesus.

Not the campaign-rally Jesus.

Not the Jesus wrapped in a flag, softened into a brand, or reduced to a symbol of national innocence.

The actual Jesus.

The Jesus who blessed the poor.

The Jesus who warned the rich.

The Jesus who touched the unclean.

The Jesus who defended the accused.

The Jesus who identified himself with the stranger, the prisoner, the sick, and the hungry.

The Jesus who exposed religious hypocrisy.

The Jesus who refused to worship power.

The Jesus who told people to love their enemies.

Would they recognize him?

Or would they call him weak?

Would they call him dangerous?

Would they call him fake?

Would they call him unmanly?

Would they call him unpatriotic?

Would they call him insulting to Jesus Christ?

Would they call him a demon?

Would they pray for his death and call it faithfulness?

That is why the attacks on James Talarico matter.

The reaction to him reveals something deeper than one Senate race.

A Christian man warns against the worship of power, and power calls him weak.

A Christian man talks about neighbors, and nationalism calls him dangerous.

A Christian man answers hatred with love, and domination does not recognize what it is seeing.

That should trouble Christians more than it does.

Because if a movement cannot recognize Christlike fruit in an imperfect human being, what makes it so sure it would recognize Christ?

This is a call to stop worshiping domination.

To stop confusing cruelty with courage.

To stop mistaking humiliation for truth.

To stop treating mercy as weakness just because it comes from outside your tribe.

To ask why Trump’s performance of strength feels more Christian to so many people than Talarico’s response of love.

That question is not merely partisan.

It is spiritual.

Because Christianity is not measured by how loudly someone says “God.”

It is not measured by how often a ruler praises the Bible.

It is not measured by how much power a movement can seize in the name of faith.

It is measured, according to Jesus, by fruit.

By love.

By mercy.

By truth.

By what happens to the least of these.

By whether the stranger is welcomed or hunted.

By whether the prisoner is remembered or discarded.

By whether the poor are blessed or blamed.

By whether the sick are cared for or abandoned.

By whether the enemy is loved or marked for destruction.

By whether the cross is remembered as the exposure of empire, or used as decoration for empire’s return.

And by whether the people who claim Christ can still recognize him when he arrives without flattering their side.

That is the crisis.

American Christianity does not lack influence.

It lacks recognition.

It can recognize the ruler.

It can recognize the weapon.

It can recognize the flag.

It can recognize the performance of strength.

It can recognize the machinery of power.

It can recognize Rome.

But can it recognize Christ?

Can it recognize him in the stranger?

Can it recognize him in the poor?

Can it recognize him in the prisoner?

Can it recognize him in the sick?

Can it recognize him in the merciful?

Can it recognize him in the hated opponent who refuses to hate back?

Because if it cannot, then the problem is not that Christianity has been pushed out of American politics.

The problem is much worse.

Christianity is everywhere.

Christ is the one they keep missing.
