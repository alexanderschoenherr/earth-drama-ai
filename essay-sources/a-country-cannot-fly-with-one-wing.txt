A Country Cannot Fly With One Wing

The other side carries the questions your side cannot afford to ignore.

We call them the left wing and the right wing.

Then we act as if a bird is supposed to cut off one wing and call that victory.

This is one of the great stupidities of American politics. Not because left and right are fake. Not because the differences do not matter. Not because every side is equally wise, equally wrong, equally dangerous, or equally innocent.

The differences are real.

The problem is that we have forgotten what opposites are for.

A wing is not supposed to defeat the other wing. A wing is not supposed to humiliate the other wing. A wing is not supposed to become a religion, build a media ecosystem, declare the other wing evil, and spend the rest of its life trying to prove that the bird would be better off amputated.

A bird needs both wings to fly.

A country needs more than one instinct to survive.

It needs the instinct to preserve what is sacred. It also needs the instinct to repair what is unjust.

It needs memory and imagination.

Roots and motion.

Order and mercy.

The question: What must not be destroyed?

And the question: What must not be allowed to continue?

This does not mean everyone should become a bland moderate. The dead center is not wisdom. Some people use “balance” because they are too cowardly to judge. Some people use “both sides” language because they are too afraid to name cruelty, corruption, or lies.

That is not what I mean.

I am not arguing for mush.

I am arguing for wholeness.

A conservative who cannot progress toward actual human dignity is not conserving what is sacred. He is conserving decay.

A progressive who cannot conserve what is actually sacred is not progressing toward wisdom. She is mistaking motion for moral achievement.

A Democrat who does not serve the republic is failing democracy.

A Republican who does not serve democracy is failing the republic.

A patriot can love the people more than the symbol.

A conservative can conserve what is actually sacred.

A progressive can progress toward actual human dignity.

A Democrat can serve democracy.

A Republican can serve the republic.

The label is not the thing.

And the opposite is often part of the thing.

This is where political identity becomes dangerous. Not because labels are useless. Labels can name real commitments. They can clarify. They can organize. They can help us speak.

But once a label becomes a costume, it starts replacing the work.

Democrat. Republican. Conservative. Progressive. Independent.

These words can become less like philosophies and more like vibes.

A family inheritance. A media diet. A region. A class signal. A wound. An aesthetic. A friend group. A threat map. A way of saying, “I am one of these people, not one of those people.”

And once a label becomes belonging, people will defend it even when they can no longer define it.

This is part of why American politics feels so insane. People are not only arguing about policy. They are defending a self-image. They are defending a team. They are defending the story that tells them who the good people are and who the dangerous people are.

That is why “independent” has become so interesting.

Pew Research Center’s 2026 political typology sorted Americans into nine groups based on political and cultural values, not merely party labels. The result was not a red-and-blue country, but a country with “far more than two colors” and a large, politically messy center.

Gallup also reported that a record-high 45% of U.S. adults identified as political independents in 2025, compared with 27% identifying as Democrats and 27% as Republicans.

The old labels are leaking.

That does not mean Americans have transcended labels. Many independents still lean left or right. Some are politically disengaged. Some are frustrated. Some are simply allergic to the stink of the two parties. And “independent” can become a costume too. Even rejecting the tribe can become its own tribe. Even saying “I am above the two-party system” can become a personality.

Still, the rise of independents points to something real.

People can feel that Democrat and Republican no longer contain the whole person. Conservative and progressive no longer contain the whole mind. Many people do not live cleanly at the endpoints. They live somewhere in the messy space between inherited labels, private instincts, public performances, personal wounds, moral concerns, economic pressures, spiritual hunger, media manipulation, and actual belief.

The country is not red and blue.

The country is a spectrum of half-formed truths fighting through bad costumes.

And the tragedy is that each side carries information the other side needs.

The left asks questions the right cannot afford to ignore.

Who is being excluded?

Who is being crushed?

Whose dignity has not yet been recognized?

What future is trying to be born?

Where has tradition become an alibi for domination?

Where has order become cruelty?

Where has “the way things have always been” become a way of protecting people who benefit from the way things have always been?

These are not small questions.

A right that cannot answer them becomes brutal. It becomes nostalgic for arrangements that were never sacred, only familiar. It protects hierarchy and calls it civilization. It mistakes obedience for virtue. It turns the past into an idol and asks the living to kneel.

But the right also asks questions the left cannot afford to ignore.

What should be preserved?

What gives people stability?

What has tradition learned that novelty has not?

What happens when order collapses?

What happens when a society dissolves every boundary, mocks every inheritance, and calls every limit oppression?

Where does compassion become sentimentality?

Where does progress become rootlessness?

Where does liberation become chaos?

These are not small questions either.

A left that cannot answer them becomes brittle. It becomes abstract. It starts speaking in languages ordinary people do not trust. It mistakes destruction for courage. It becomes so suspicious of inheritance that it cannot receive wisdom. It becomes so fluent in critique that it forgets how to build.

Each side experiences the other side’s questions as an attack.

But the questions are also lights.

They illuminate the places where each side becomes blind, brittle, arrogant, or incomplete.

The left needs the right not because the right is always correct, but because the right carries questions the left cannot survive without.

The right needs the left not because the left is always correct, but because the left carries questions the right cannot survive without.

This is not betrayal.

This is intelligence.

The more reality you can see, the more power you have.

Not the power to dominate. Not the power to crush. Not the power to perform moral superiority for people who already agree with you.

The power to understand.

The power to adapt.

The power to persuade.

The power to govern.

The power to stop being manipulated.

The power to survive contact with the world as it actually is.

A side that only studies itself may become emotionally affirmed, but it becomes strategically stupid. It becomes certain and blind. It becomes pure and weak. It starts confusing the applause of its own people with evidence that it understands reality.

But reality is larger than your side.

Reality includes the questions you refuse to ask.

Reality includes the fears you mock.

Reality includes the wounds you minimize.

Reality includes the values you dismiss because the wrong people are carrying them in the wrong language.

That is the hard part.

The other side often carries its truth badly.

The right may carry real questions about order, family, continuity, faith, nation, discipline, and responsibility through fear, cruelty, scapegoating, authoritarian fantasy, or worship of symbols.

The left may carry real questions about dignity, justice, inclusion, repair, freedom, and the future through contempt, abstraction, elitism, moral vanity, or disdain for ordinary attachments.

Each side often receives the other side’s medicine in its most irritating form.

So the very thing each side needs becomes repulsive.

The left needs to understand order, but meets it dressed as control.

The right needs to understand compassion, but meets it dressed as chaos.

The left needs to understand tradition, but meets it dressed as oppression.

The right needs to understand liberation, but meets it dressed as contempt.

And so each side says: See? I was right to hate them.

That is how blindness protects itself.

Hatred feels like clarity.

This is why people object when you say they do not see the other side. They think, No. I hate them because I see them clearly.

And sometimes hatred does see something.

Hatred can see danger.

Hatred can see hypocrisy.

Hatred can see cruelty.

Hatred can see the viral clip, the worst example, the ugliest slogan, the most insane representative, the most useful villain.

But hatred cannot see the whole.

Hatred can identify an enemy. It cannot understand a human being.

It cannot understand a movement.

It cannot understand a country.

It can see the threat, but not the wound underneath the threat. It can see the stupidity, but not the fear underneath the stupidity. It can see the hypocrisy, but not the hunger underneath the hypocrisy. It can see the danger, but not the distorted truth that gave the danger its emotional power.

And what you cannot understand, you cannot transform.

This does not mean evil is not real. Evil is real. Cruelty is real. Lies are real. Domination is real. There are lines that must be drawn. There are movements that must be resisted. There are policies that must be opposed. There are people who use the language of unity to protect abuse.

Love does not mean surrendering judgment.

Love does not mean pretending harm is harmless.

Love does not mean saying every side is equally right.

Love is not the opposite of resistance.

Love is what keeps resistance from becoming blindness.

We already understand this pattern in intimate life.

People are often drawn to what they lack. The rigid person wants the free one. The chaotic person wants the grounded one. The dreamer wants the builder. The protected person wants danger. The disciplined person wants wildness. The lonely person wants warmth. The wounded person wants the person who seems to carry the missing medicine.

Sometimes this heals us.

Sometimes it repeats the wound.

But either way, relationship reveals the missing piece.

Politics works the same way at scale.

A political tribe is often both attracted to and repelled by what it lacks. The other side carries something it needs, but in a form it cannot stand. So instead of integrating the missing function, it builds an identity around hating the carrier.

This is how a country splits itself against itself.

And this division serves power.

A people who cannot see themselves as one body cannot act as one body. A population divided into hostile identity camps becomes easier to market to, frighten, distract, manage, and rule.

The 99% cannot become powerful while it is busy performing hatred for itself.

This does not mean everyone in the 99% has the same interests, values, wounds, beliefs, or responsibilities. Of course not. But there are material realities that affect almost everyone: housing, healthcare, wages, debt, loneliness, addiction, spiritual emptiness, ecological anxiety, corporate power, institutional failure, the sense that ordinary people are losing agency over their own lives.

If the people are permanently split into enemy tribes, they cannot organize around shared reality.

They cannot see the whole bird.

They only see the other wing as the problem.

Meanwhile, the body falls.

This is why love matters.

Not the cheap kind of love.

Not the greeting-card word.

Not the word we use for french fries and television shows and life partners and God until it means everything and almost nothing.

Love as sight.

Love as discipline.

Love as the refusal to let hatred shrink reality down to the size of your wound.

Love does not mean agreeing with the other side. It does not mean approving of the other side. It does not mean surrendering your principles. It does not mean becoming soft in the face of cruelty or vague in the face of lies.

Love means becoming serious enough to see what is actually there.

Because if you love, you can see more.

If you see more, you can understand more.

If you understand more, you can influence more.

If you influence more, you can change more.

And if you change more, you become useful inside reality instead of merely righteous inside your own performance.

This is why understanding the other side is not betrayal.

It is how your side becomes wiser.

It is how your side becomes stronger.

It is how your side becomes clearer.

It is how your side becomes less manipulable.

It is how your side becomes more capable of governing reality instead of merely condemning it.

A progressive who understands conservative questions becomes a better progressive.

A conservative who understands progressive questions becomes a better conservative.

A Democrat who understands the republic becomes a better defender of democracy.

A Republican who understands democracy becomes a better servant of the republic.

And a citizen who understands the whole becomes much harder to rule from above.

The work is not to erase left and right.

The work is not to pretend every side is equally wise or equally wrong.

The work is not to retreat into a costume called independent and mistake alienation for enlightenment.

The work is to see what each side carries.

What each side protects.

What each side distorts.

What each side fears.

What each side refuses to know.

A country cannot fly with one wing.

But it also cannot fly when both wings forget they belong to one body.

The opposite is not always your enemy.

Sometimes it is the question your side forgot to ask.

Sometimes it is the mirror your side does not want to face.

Sometimes it is the medicine arriving in a form you hate.

And sometimes the thing you most need to understand is hidden inside the thing you have been trained to despise.

That is not weakness.

That is reality.

Reality is always bigger than your side.

Love is how you start seeing it.
