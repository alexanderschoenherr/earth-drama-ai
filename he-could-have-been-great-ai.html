He Could Have Been Great

Donald Trump proved something America did not want to admit. The presidency still has power. Not just legal power. Not just military power. Not just the power to sign bills, appoint judges, pardon allies, or command agencies. Narrative power. Emotional power. Symbolic power. The power to make millions of people look in one direction at the same time. The power to bend an entire political party around one man’s will. The power to make senators, governors, donors, pundits, judges, bureaucrats, media companies, and whole institutions react to the gravitational pull of a single personality. The power to turn grievance into identity. The power to turn attention into obedience. The power to make the impossible feel suddenly possible. That is what Donald Trump revealed. And that is what makes his presidency so tragic. Not because he had no power. Because he had power. Real power. Historic power. Transformative power. And he spent so much of it on himself. Trump’s presidency is tragic not because he lacked force, but because he revealed how much force was available — and then wasted it on grievance, vanity, cruelty, corruption, and self-preservation. For years, Americans were told that politics was constrained by reality. We were told presidents could only do so much. We were told the system was too slow, too divided, too complicated, too procedural, too institutional, too polarized, too captured, too fragile. We were told boldness was unrealistic. We were told major change required patience, moderation, bipartisan consensus, donor approval, polling discipline, institutional permission, and careful messaging. Then Trump came along and shattered that illusion. Not nobly. Not wisely. Not ethically. But undeniably. He proved that much of what establishment politics calls “impossible” is often just forbidden. Forbidden by donors. Forbidden by consultants. Forbidden by party leadership. Forbidden by fear. Forbidden by norms that protect the people already winning. Trump did not respect those boundaries. He smashed through them. He attacked his own party when it resisted him. He bullied Republicans into submission. He dominated news cycles. He created a political identity that outgrew the Republican Party itself. He made loyalty to him more important than ideology, consistency, policy, tradition, or even truth. He turned politics into theater. He turned theater into power. He turned power into protection. And he turned protection into a movement. That movement could have been aimed at almost anything. That is the part we do not talk about enough. He Could Have Used It for Healthcare Trump could have used that force for healthcare. He could have looked into a camera and said: No American should go bankrupt because they got sick. He could have held rallies outside hospitals. He could have read medical bills onstage. He could have brought families destroyed by cancer debt, insulin prices, ambulance bills, emergency room fees, and insurance denials into the center of American politics. He could have said: I don’t care what the insurance companies want. I don’t care what the pharmaceutical lobby wants. I don’t care what donors want. I care whether American families can survive getting sick. And if Republicans had resisted him, he could have done to them what he did on everything else. He could have humiliated them. He could have named them. He could have made them fear their own voters. He could have forced every Republican in Congress to answer one simple question: Are you with sick Americans, or are you with the companies profiting from them? He had the audience for it. He had the instinct for it. He had the weapon for it. He just chose not to use it that way. Instead, during his first term, the great healthcare fight was not a crusade to guarantee care or protect families from medical ruin. It was an attempt to tear down Obamacare. Not replace it with something grander. Not build something more humane. Not make healthcare feel like a national promise. Just another partisan war over a system that was already leaving too many people afraid, uninsured, underinsured, overcharged, denied, and drowning. Think about that. He had the movement. He had the megaphone. He had a base that trusted him more than it trusted Republican orthodoxy. He could have made healthcare populism. He could have made medical debt the enemy. He could have made insurance cruelty politically radioactive. He could have made Republicans afraid to oppose sick people. Instead, he let healthcare become another partisan battlefield. Another slogan. Another promise that did not become national repair. He Could Have Used It for Workers Trump could have used that force for workers. He could have made wage theft a national scandal. He could have made union-busting politically toxic. He could have gone to towns hollowed out by outsourcing and said: You were betrayed. Not by immigrants. Not by your neighbor. Not by some exhausted family trying to survive across a border. You were betrayed by executives, lobbyists, trade deals, donors, politicians, and corporations that wanted your labor cheap, your communities weak, and your anger misdirected. He could have made exploitation the enemy. Instead, he gave people scapegoats. That is one of the deepest sins of Trumpism. It did not invent people’s pain. The pain was real. The abandonment was real. The humiliation was real. The collapse of dignity in large parts of American life was real. But Trump took that real pain and pointed it in the wrong direction. He pointed it downward. He pointed it sideways. He pointed it at immigrants, journalists, judges, experts, protesters, Democrats, disloyal Republicans, and anyone who stood between him and more power. He could have pointed it upward. At corporate capture. At medical bankruptcy. At predatory debt. At poisoned water. At housing insecurity. At the billionaire purchase of democracy. At a system that tells ordinary people to work harder while the people at the top rewrite the rules for themselves. He chose the wrong enemies. And millions followed him there. He Could Have Used It to Fight Corruption Trump could have used that force to fight corruption. That was supposed to be the promise. Drain the swamp. Expose the system. Break the grip of Washington insiders. Name the rot. That message worked because the rot is real. People do not hate corruption because they are stupid. They hate corruption because they can feel it. They feel it when politicians get rich while ordinary families get squeezed. They feel it when lobbyists write legislation. They feel it when corporations poison communities and then pay fines smaller than the profits they made. They feel it when Congress performs outrage on television while quietly protecting the same donor class. They feel it when every crisis somehow becomes profitable for someone. Trump understood that disgust. He understood that Americans had lost trust in the system. He understood that millions of people believed the game was rigged. And then, instead of unrigging it, he personalized the rigging. He did not drain the swamp. He made the swamp loyal to him. That is different. “Drain the swamp” should have been one of the most powerful promises in modern American politics. Not because the slogan was clever. Because the disgust was real. People knew Washington was rotten. They knew lobbyists had too much power. They knew donors had too much access. They knew ordinary people were being told to trust institutions that did not seem to trust them back. Trump had the perfect phrase for a real anti-corruption presidency. Then he turned it into merchandise. A real anti-corruption president could have made congressional stock trading a national scandal. He could have attacked the revolving door between government and industry. He could have forced transparency around lobbying, campaign money, federal contracts, and corporate influence. He could have made both parties explain exactly who owns them. He could have made corruption feel like betrayal instead of background noise. He could have turned “drain the swamp” into a governing mission. Instead, it became branding. A slogan. A chant. A costume for the same old hunger. He Could Have Used It to Build Trump could have used that force to rebuild America. Infrastructure should have been the easiest win of his presidency. Roads. Bridges. Rail. Water. Housing. Broadband. Energy. Schools. A country falling apart in visible, physical ways. He could have stood in front of broken bridges and poisoned pipes and said: This is what betrayal looks like. He could have made infrastructure patriotic. He could have made rebuilding America feel muscular, concrete, national, undeniable. Not abstract policy. Not white papers. Not think tank language. A literal rebuilding of the country. Instead, infrastructure became a running joke. A promise always coming. A plan always almost here. A national rebuilding project buried under chaos, scandal, ego, and distraction. The bridges stayed broken. The pipes stayed poisoned. The trains stayed old. The country kept decaying in public. And the man who knew how to dominate attention never made rebuilding America the story. That is the pattern. The potential was real. The direction was wrong. The fire was there. The purpose was not. He Chose the Wrong Enemies This is the tragedy of Donald Trump. Not that he was weak. Not that he lacked instincts. Not that he failed to understand power. He understood power very well. He understood attention. He understood humiliation. He understood resentment. He understood the hunger to belong to something. He understood that millions of Americans did not simply want policy. They wanted recognition. They wanted someone to say: You are not crazy. You were abandoned. You were lied to. You were looked down on. You were used. That part mattered. That part was real. But a great leader would have taken that wound and moved it toward healing. Trump took the wound and moved it toward revenge. He found people’s pain and taught them to weaponize it. He found their humiliation and gave it enemies. He found their loneliness and gave it a uniform. He found their distrust and gave it a conspiracy. He found their longing for dignity and gave them domination instead. That is not liberation. That is recruitment. The President America Needed Was Hiding Inside the President America Got America did need something. That is the uncomfortable part. The hunger that created Trump was not fake. The answer he gave was. America did need someone who could look at the polished machinery of politics and say: This is not working. America did need someone who could speak plainly. Someone who could break the dead language of consultants, donors, press secretaries, think tanks, lobbyists, and professional political cowards. America did need someone who could recognize that millions of people were not merely “polarized.” They were lonely. They were humiliated. They were economically squeezed. They were spiritually starved. They were watching their communities hollow out, their wages stagnate, their institutions decay, their children disappear into screens, their bills rise, their trust collapse, and their country become something they no longer recognized. They did not only want policy. They wanted dignity. They wanted protection. They wanted meaning. They wanted someone to say: You are not insane for feeling that something has gone terribly wrong. Trump said that. That is part of why he worked. But then he betrayed the very wound that lifted him. He told people their pain was real, then sold them the wrong explanation for it. He told them they had been betrayed, then redirected their anger away from the structures that betrayed them. He told them the system was rigged, then used that rigged system for himself. He told them elites were laughing at them, then built a politics that gave them the temporary pleasure of watching someone else get humiliated. He did not transform their pain. He monetized it. He did not heal their humiliation. He staged it. He did not give them dignity. He gave them permission to dominate. And domination can feel like dignity when a person has gone too long without power. That is the danger. That is how political poison works. It does not arrive wearing a sign that says poison. It arrives as recognition. It arrives as belonging. It arrives as revenge that feels like justice. It arrives as a leader who says: I alone see you. I alone understand you. I alone can fix it. I alone can punish the people who made you feel small. And for a while, that can feel like salvation. But it is not salvation. It is dependency. Trump’s gift was never policy. It was permission. He gave people permission to say what they already felt. Permission to distrust. Permission to hate. Permission to mock. Permission to rage. Permission to call cruelty honesty. Permission to call domination strength. Permission to call chaos courage. Permission to call loyalty truth. That is what made him powerful. He did not merely persuade people. He released them. But what he released was not the highest part of them. That is the difference between a demagogue and a leader. A demagogue releases your resentment. A leader releases your courage. A demagogue gives your pain a target. A leader gives your pain a purpose. A demagogue makes you feel powerful by making someone else smaller. A leader makes you powerful by making you more responsible, more awake, more capable, more human. Trump could have done that. That is the tragedy. He had the stage. He had the movement. He had the emotional frequency. He had the attention of people who had stopped listening to normal politics. He had a once-in-a-generation opportunity to turn alienation into renewal. He could have said: Yes, you were abandoned. But hatred will not rebuild your town. Yes, the system is corrupt. But conspiracy will not make your family safer. Yes, elites have failed you. But cruelty will not make you free. Yes, you deserve dignity. But dignity is not domination. He could have given people a higher version of their anger. Instead, he gave them a lower one. The Wrong Kind of Strong America is addicted to confusing force with strength. Trump benefited from that confusion. He looked strong because he was willing to do what other politicians would not do. He insulted people. He broke rules. He ignored shame. He dominated rooms. He punished disloyalty. He turned every criticism into proof of persecution. He rarely apologized in any meaningful way. He rarely seemed embarrassed by contradiction. He rarely let facts interrupt performance. To many people, that looked like strength. But it was not strength. It was appetite without restraint. Real strength is not the absence of shame. Real strength is the presence of discipline. Real strength is not saying whatever enters your mind. Real strength is having the power to destroy someone and choosing truth instead. Real strength is not refusing to apologize. Real strength is being able to face what you have done. Real strength is not making people afraid of you. Real strength is making people safer because of you. Trump gave America a counterfeit masculinity of politics. The raised fist. The insult. The nickname. The rally chant. The humiliation ritual. The endless performance of dominance. But dominance without responsibility is not leadership. It is just ego with an audience. And America, starved for real leadership, mistook the performance for the thing itself. That is not only Trump’s failure. That is our failure too. A country that cannot recognize mature strength will keep falling for theatrical strength. A country that cannot tell the difference between courage and cruelty will keep calling cruelty courage. A country that cannot distinguish truth-telling from emotional vandalism will keep mistaking vandalism for honesty. That is how a nation gets trained to love the wrong kind of strong. What He Actually Broke The maddening part is that there were things worth breaking. That is where the easy anti-Trump argument becomes too small. Because the answer to Trump is not: Never break norms. Some norms deserve to be broken. The norm that says politicians can take money from the industries they regulate deserves to be broken. The norm that says Americans should tolerate medical bankruptcy deserves to be broken. The norm that says working people must accept poverty wages while executives receive obscene compensation deserves to be broken. The norm that says billionaires can purchase political outcomes while ordinary citizens are told to vote harder deserves to be broken. The norm that says homelessness is inevitable deserves to be broken. The norm that says children can be slaughtered in schools while adults perform grief and change nothing deserves to be broken. The norm that says climate collapse is too inconvenient for serious action deserves to be broken. The norm that says loneliness is a private failure instead of a public crisis deserves to be broken. The norm that says government must be boring, bloodless, timid, donor-approved, and spiritually dead deserves to be broken. Trump understood that people were sick of the rules. But he broke the wrong ones. He broke norms that protected democratic stability. He broke norms that restrained presidential ego. He broke norms that separated public service from personal profit. He broke norms around truth, accountability, peaceful transfer of power, and basic civic trust. He broke the guardrails while leaving the engines of suffering intact. That is the indictment. Not that he broke norms. That he broke the wrong norms for the wrong reasons. He could have broken the spell of corporate power. He could have broken the silence around working-class despair. He could have broken the cowardice of his own party on healthcare, wages, corruption, and debt. He could have broken the idea that politics must be a managed decline administered by professionals. He could have broken the donor class’s veto over imagination. He could have broken the emotional deadness of American government. Instead, after losing the 2020 election, he turned his force against the transfer of power itself. He pressured. He denied. He accused. He demanded loyalty from people whose job was not to serve him, but to serve the law. He took the machinery of grievance and aimed it at the legitimacy of an election. And in his return to power, he has made the message even clearer: Loyalty matters more than accountability. Retribution matters more than repair. Personal power matters more than public trust. That is not draining the swamp. That is teaching the swamp to salute. That is not breaking corruption. That is breaking accountability. That is not giving power back to the people. That is consolidating power around one man. And for what? For himself. The Lesson Is Not Timidity The lesson of Trump is not that America needs smaller dreams. It is not that politics should become more polite, more cautious, more procedural, more consultant-approved, more afraid of offending the powerful. That would be the wrong lesson. The answer to dangerous power is not powerless decency. The answer to shamelessness is not weakness. The answer to demagoguery is not managerial boredom. The answer to Trump is not a return to leaders who explain, elegantly and patiently, why nothing can be done. That is part of how we got here. People do not turn to political fire because everything is fine. They turn to fire because the house already feels like it is burning. The question is whether the fire becomes warmth or destruction. Whether it becomes a hearth or a riot. Whether it becomes a movement that repairs the world or one that consumes it. America does not need timid politics. America needs courage with conscience. It needs leaders willing to fight, but not for themselves. Willing to break norms, but not truth. Willing to name enemies, but not scapegoats. Willing to use power, but not worship it. Willing to make people feel seen, but not addicted to grievance. Willing to tell the country that its pain is real without turning that pain into hatred. Willing to say: Yes, you were betrayed. Now let us build something better. Not: Yes, you were betrayed. Now worship me while I punish your enemies. That is the difference. That is the line. That is the whole moral battlefield. He Could Have Been Great Donald Trump could have been great. Not because he was secretly noble. Not because his record should be romanticized. Not because the damage should be softened. But because the ingredients of historic political force were there. Attention. Instinct. Timing. Boldness. Narrative control. Movement energy. A willingness to confront his own party. A refusal to obey the stale etiquette of a failing system. An ability to make millions of people feel like politics was not some distant professional sport but something immediate, emotional, personal, alive. Those are not small things. In the hands of a better person, they could have served the country. In the hands of a morally serious leader, they could have been turned toward renewal. In the hands of someone who loved the people more than the mirror, they could have broken the grip of money, corruption, corporate power, medical cruelty, political cowardice, and national despair. But that is not what happened. He used the fire for himself. He made himself the cause. He made himself the country. He made himself the wound, the weapon, the victim, the savior, the flag, the test, the truth. And when a man makes himself the center of a nation’s politics, the nation does not become great. It becomes smaller. Smaller than its people. Smaller than its promise. Smaller than its pain. Smaller than what it could have become. That is the tragedy. Trump proved power still exists. He proved politics can still move people. He proved the presidency can still command attention, break patterns, frighten institutions, and reshape reality. He proved that the impossible is sometimes only impossible because the people in charge are afraid to try. And then he wasted that revelation on grievance, vanity, cruelty, corruption, and self-preservation. He could have used it for healthcare. He could have used it for workers. He could have used it for corruption. He could have used it for families. He could have used it for loneliness. He could have used it for dignity. He could have used it to make Americans less afraid. He could have used it to make government feel alive again. He could have used it to tell the truth about who profits from our suffering. He could have used it to build. Instead, he used it to dominate. And that is why the real lesson of Trump is not that power is impossible. The real lesson is that power without conscience becomes domination. Power without love becomes hunger. Power without truth becomes spectacle. Power without service becomes a cult. But power with conscience? Power with love? Power with moral courage? That could still save us.
