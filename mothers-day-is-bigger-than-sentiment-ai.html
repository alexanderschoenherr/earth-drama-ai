The First World On Mother’s Day, motherhood, and the love that holds us before we can hold ourselves Mother’s Day Is Bigger Than Sentiment Mother’s Day is easy to make small. We turn it into flowers, cards, brunch reservations, social media posts, and the familiar annual language of gratitude. None of that is wrong. Flowers are beautiful. Cards matter. A phone call can mean more than we know. But Mother’s Day points toward something much larger than sentiment. It points toward one of the deepest facts of human life: none of us arrived here self-made. Before we had opinions, ambitions, careers, wounds, philosophies, favorite songs, private theories, or names we could answer to, we were helpless. We were bodies. We were hunger. We were noise. We were need. And someone had to care for us before we could care for ourselves. That is the truth beneath the holiday. Under the flowers and soft language is an ancient human reality: every life begins in dependence. Every person who becomes anything first has to be held, fed, protected, soothed, watched, remembered, and kept alive. We are not born independent. We are born into arms. For many of us, those first arms were our mother’s. Not always perfectly. Not always simply. Not always without pain or complication. Mother’s Day is not an uncomplicated day for everyone. For some, it is grief. For some, absence. For some, longing. For some, a wound that still has no clean name. For some, it means missing a mother, losing a child, wanting to be a mother, being unable to become one, or trying to honor a relationship that still carries ache. A day about mothers has to be large enough to hold all of that. Maybe that is exactly why the day matters. Because motherhood, at its deepest, is not just a sweet idea. It is not just a sentimental role wrapped in flowers. It is one of the primal structures of human life. It is the place where need first meets response. Where helplessness first meets care. Where the body first learns whether the world can be warm. Motherhood is not only biology. It is the act of helping life survive until it can stand. And that work can take many forms. Some people are mothered by biological mothers. Some by grandmothers, aunts, sisters, stepmothers, adoptive mothers, fathers, teachers, neighbors, friends, or chosen family. The form can change. The function remains. Someone makes the world safer than it would have been. Someone notices. Someone stays. Someone carries what another life cannot yet carry for itself. That is not small. That is civilization before civilization has a name. None of Us Arrived Self-Made We live in a culture that loves the idea of the self-made person. We praise independence. We admire discipline. We tell stories about ambition, success, reinvention, grit, and becoming. We like to imagine the adult as the author of his own life, standing on his own feet, building himself from willpower and vision. There is truth in that. But it is not the first truth. The first truth is that every strong person was once fragile. Every independent person was once dependent. Every voice that now speaks began as a cry someone had to answer. Every body that now moves through the world had to be washed, fed, clothed, held, and watched over before it could do anything for itself. No one begins as a self. We begin as need. Before we can choose, someone chooses for us. Before we can remember, someone remembers us. Before we can regulate our own fear, someone lends us their nervous system. Before we can make meaning, someone keeps us alive long enough to discover meaning at all. That is one of the humbling truths of being human. We are not as separate as we pretend. At the root of every life is dependence. At the root of every identity is care. At the root of every future is someone’s willingness to be interrupted by another person’s need. A baby does not care if you are tired. A child does not understand what you sacrificed. A son does not know, in the moment, how much invisible work is happening around him. He simply lives inside it. He grows up inside meals, rides, reminders, clean clothes, worried glances, birthday plans, doctor’s appointments, softened rooms, answered cries, corrected mistakes, second chances, and a thousand ordinary forms of protection he may not recognize until much later. Part of growing up is realizing that love was doing work around you long before you knew how to see it. A child receives care as atmosphere. An adult begins to understand it as labor. That is one of the reasons Mother’s Day can become more powerful with age. When we are young, we may thank our mothers because we are told to. Because the calendar says so. Because the card aisle appears. Because it is what a decent person does. But as we get older, the gratitude changes. It becomes less automatic. And more devastating. Because eventually, if we are paying attention, we begin to understand that the person who cared for us was also trying to survive her own life. She had her own body, her own fears, her own limits, her own history, her own disappointments, her own exhaustion, her own private weather. She was not mothering us from outside of life. She was mothering us from inside it. No mother gets to raise a child from outside the conditions of her own existence. She mothers from inside her own body. Her own wounds. Her own resources. Her own relationships. Her own era. Her own family inheritance. Her own unanswered questions. Her own hope. Her own fatigue. Her own imperfect understanding of what love required. And still, somehow, life had to continue. The child had to be fed. The day had to be survived. The home had to be made livable again. That is the scale of motherhood that sentiment often hides. Motherhood is not beautiful because it is soft. It is beautiful because it is difficult, human, repeated, unfinished, and still offered. The First World A mother is not only someone who brings a child into the world. She is often the first way the world arrives. Before a child knows language, they know tone. Before they know morality, they know safety. Before they know history, culture, religion, money, beauty, danger, or time, they know whether the arms around them feel steady. They know the rhythm of a voice. They know the warmth of a body. They know the difference between being left alone and being returned to. A mother becomes, in those earliest days, the first atmosphere. The first shelter. The first weather. The first proof that life can answer need with care. That does not mean every mother is perfect. No mother is. It does not mean every beginning is gentle. Many are not. It does not mean every child receives what they needed in the way they needed it. Many do not. But the role itself is still enormous. To mother a life is to stand near the beginning of someone’s relationship with existence. It is to become part of the child’s first translation of the world. Before the child can say, “I am safe,” the body has already begun learning whether safety is possible. Before the child can say, “I am loved,” the nervous system has already begun learning whether love returns. That is why motherhood reaches so far beyond the visible years. A mother is not simply remembered. She is absorbed. She becomes part of the inner climate. The tone a person hears when they are alone. The way they respond to tenderness. The way they receive worry. The way they trust or brace. The way they hope someone will come back. To be mothered is to learn, before language, that life can hold us. And even when that holding is imperfect, even when it is tangled with fear, exhaustion, misunderstanding, or pain, the attempt itself matters. The returning matters. The trying matters. The hand reaching again matters. Because a child does not only need the world to be flawless. A child needs the world to keep coming back. That is one of the quiet powers of mothers and mothering figures. They return. They try again. They repair. They make another meal. They answer another call. They notice another change in the face, another silence in the room, another hidden worry moving through the child’s body. They become the first evidence that life is not only something that happens to us. Life can also come toward us with care. The Labor Beneath the Love Much of what mothers do disappears into the ordinary miracle of everyone else being okay. The appointment was remembered. The meal was made. The shirt was washed. The birthday was planned. The medicine was picked up. The feeling was noticed. The silence was interpreted. The worry was sensed before anyone else knew there was something to worry about. And because the day continues, the labor vanishes. That is one of the strange injustices of care: when it succeeds, it often becomes invisible. The child made it to school. The home stayed warm. The family kept moving. The crisis was softened. The birthday felt special. The refrigerator was not empty. The small wound did not become a larger one. So everyone calls it normal. But normal is often someone’s work. A mother often becomes the memory of the family, carrying details everyone else is free to forget. She remembers who likes what, who needs a call, who is sensitive about which subject, who has been quiet lately, what appointment is coming, what pain is old, what story still matters, what small gesture might make someone feel loved. I think of my own mother, Cherie, and the way she remembers birthdays and conversations, the way she stores little pieces of people as proof that they matter. I think of her in the kitchen, cooking, laughing, filling the room with joy, making service feel less like obligation and more like love taking physical form. Her love language has always been serving others. Not as performance. Not as martyrdom. As presence. A meal. A reminder. A word of encouragement. A concern she cannot quite put down. A voice that still wants to know if the people she loves are safe. That is the kind of love that can look ordinary from the outside and enormous once you finally understand it. A mother carries the calendar. She carries the atmosphere. She carries the emotional weather. Sometimes she changes the temperature of a room without saying much at all. Sometimes she knows something is wrong before anyone admits it. Sometimes she holds the family together not through grand heroic gestures, but through tiny acts of continuity repeated so often that people stop seeing them. A ride. A look. A question. A hand on the shoulder. A text that says, “Did you get home?” A mother’s love is often made of things too ordinary to be praised and too necessary to be absent. That is why the gratitude can come late. As children, we may not understand the cost of being cared for. We do not see the tiredness behind the patience. We do not see the fear beneath the protection. We do not see the private grief that still had to make dinner. We do not see the woman trying to hold herself together while holding everyone else. We only know that the world kept functioning. And then, if we are lucky or honest or old enough, we begin to understand: the world did not keep functioning by accident. Someone was tending it. Someone was making it livable. Someone was carrying what would have collapsed if no one carried it. That is the labor beneath the love. The Woman Before the Mother One of the strange lessons of adulthood is realizing that your mother did not begin when you did. As children, we often experience our mothers as functions of our own lives. She is Mom. She is the person who answers, drives, cooks, reminds, worries, loves, corrects, comforts, notices, and appears. She is the person we call when something happens. The person whose existence seems somehow attached to ours. But before she was “Mom,” she was herself. A girl. A daughter. A young woman. A private universe of hopes, fears, beauty, questions, griefs, jokes, dreams, disappointments, longings, and moments no child can fully imagine. She had a face before we knew it as our mother’s face. She had a name before we called her Mom. She had days that had nothing to do with us. Sorrows we did not witness. Joys we did not cause. Choices we were not there to understand. Versions of herself that existed before our lives entered hers and rearranged everything. That realization can be humbling. Because it means motherhood is not only the story of what a mother gives to a child. It is also the story of a woman continuing to exist while giving. A woman becoming and mothering at the same time. A woman learning how to care for another life without disappearing completely inside the caring. And perhaps one of the deepest ways to honor a mother is not only to thank her for what she did, but to remember that she is someone beyond what she did for us. She is not only the meals she made. Not only the rides she gave. Not only the worry she carried. Not only the sacrifices we understand or the mistakes she regrets. Not only the role. Not only the word Mother. She is a whole human being. And maybe that is where Mother’s Day can become more honest, more beautiful, and more complete. Not by making mothers larger than life, but by allowing them to be fully alive inside it. Not saints. Not symbols. People. Women with histories. Women with wounds. Women with humor. Women with beauty. Women with old dreams and new dreams. Women who gave life while still trying to understand their own. I think, too, of my mother as a daughter. I think of her loving her own mother in her last days. That image matters to me because it reveals something Mother’s Day can easily forget: mothers are also children. They, too, have stood beside aging parents, carried grief, offered tenderness, and learned what love costs by giving it when it is hardest to give. Before my mother was the woman who cared for me, she was also someone’s child trying to love well. That humbles me. It makes her more real. And it makes my gratitude deeper. To see a mother clearly is not to diminish her. It is to honor her more completely. Because the love means more when we remember it came from a person, not a myth. Love Looking Backward There is another thing many mothers carry, and it is harder to see. Guilt. Not the shallow kind. Not the performative kind. The private kind. The kind that lives in the quiet places of a person’s heart. The guilt over what they did. What they did not do. What they noticed too late. What they misunderstood. What they were too tired to handle perfectly. What they wish they had protected. What they wish they had said differently. What they wish they had known sooner. A mother can spend years loving a child and still look backward with ache. Still wonder if she gave enough. Still replay old moments with new understanding. Still ask herself questions no one else may even know she is carrying. I think about this because my own mother recently told me she struggles with that kind of guilt. I will not turn her private heart into a public confession. But I will say that hearing it changed the way I think about Mother’s Day. Because many mothers, I think, carry some version of that private courtroom inside them. They sit before old memories. Old choices. Old limits. Old versions of themselves. Old moments they wish they could enter again with more wisdom, more softness, more strength, more patience, more protection, more knowledge. But hindsight can be cruel. It gives the present self a map the past self did not have. It lets a mother see clearly now what she could not see clearly then. It asks her to judge old moments with new eyes, old choices with new information, old exhaustion with a rested mind, old fear with the safety of distance. A mother’s guilt is often love looking backward, trying to revise what can no longer be changed. It is love with hindsight. Love with ache. Love standing in the ruins of old moments, wishing it could go back with everything it knows now. And sometimes, yes, guilt points toward something real. Some mothers made mistakes. Some children were hurt. Some things should have been different. Love does not require us to lie about that. But guilt is rarely clean. Some of it belongs to real regret. Some of it belongs to impossible standards. Some of it belongs to circumstances no one could fully control. Some of it belongs to the terrible fact that human beings do not get to live life with the knowledge they only gain afterward. No mother raises a child from outside the conditions of her own life. She mothers from inside her own body, her own wounds, her own fear, her own resources, her own relationships, her own era, her own exhaustion, her own unfinished healing. She mothers without knowing the ending. She mothers while becoming. And maybe one of the most loving things an adult child can say is not, “Nothing ever hurt.” Sometimes things did hurt. Maybe the more honest sentence is: “I know you were human.” I know you were carrying things too. I know you did not always know what I needed. I know love can be real and still imperfect. I know care can miss the mark and still have been care. I know you may look back and see failures, but I also see effort. I see endurance. I see the ways you kept trying. I see the ways you stayed. And I do not need you to have been perfect in order to honor you. Maybe that is part of what Mother’s Day can become when we let it grow up. Not a day that turns mothers into saints. Not a day that forces children to pretend every wound is healed. But a day large enough to hold the truth: that mothers are people. That love is labor. That care is imperfect. That guilt is sometimes grief wearing the face of responsibility. And that many mothers who wonder whether their love was enough are asking that question because their love is still alive in them, still reaching backward, still trying to bless what it cannot change. Two Kinds of Gratitude I think about this every year because my own mother’s birthday falls close to Mother’s Day. The timing has always felt quietly meaningful to me, as if the calendar itself is asking me to hold two kinds of gratitude at once. Mother’s Day asks me to thank her for giving me life. Her birthday asks me to thank life for giving her. One honors the role. The other honors the woman. And the older I get, the more important that distinction becomes. Because I do not only want to thank my mother for what she gave me. I want to thank existence for the fact that she exists at all. Before she was my mother, she was Cherie. A life. A soul. A person moving through the world with her own story, her own tenderness, her own ache, her own laughter, her own becoming. Her birthday reminds me that she was born before she became the person who would help me be born. That feels sacred to me. Mother’s Day is gratitude moving in one direction: from the child toward the mother who carried him. But a birthday widens the gratitude. It reaches farther back. It says: before you carried me, something carried you. Before you gave me life, life gave you yours. Before I could thank you, the world had already received you. So when these days come close together, I feel the two truths touching. Thank you for giving me life. And thank God, thank time, thank the universe, thank every mysterious turn of history, that you were given life too. Because if she had not been born, I would not be here. But more than that: if she had not been born, the world would be missing the particular shape of her. Her voice. Her presence. Her way of caring. Her way of remembering. Her way of worrying. Her way of loving. Her way of being Cherie. The Cherie who says, “Praise the Lord. Praise Jesus. Hallelujah.” The Cherie who worries about things she cannot control because love, in her, does not know how to be indifferent. The Cherie who wants her son to find true love. The Cherie who remembers birthdays, remembers conversations, and keeps pieces of people alive by refusing to forget what matters to them. The Cherie who serves. The Cherie who encourages. The Cherie who laughs with her family in the kitchen and fills ordinary rooms with joy. Mother’s Day can make a mother sound like a role. A birthday returns her to personhood. It says: before we celebrate what you did for others, we celebrate that you are. That may be the deeper gift I want to give my mother this year. Not only gratitude for the mother she has been. But reverence for the woman she is. A Blessing for the Ones Who Carry Life So yes, give the flowers. Make the call. Write the card. Take her to lunch. Send the text. Say the thing while there is still time to say it. But underneath the gesture, remember the larger truth: every human life begins as a life being held. Every person who becomes anything first had to survive being nothing but need. Before we could stand, someone had to lift us. Before we could speak, someone had to answer us. Before we could become ourselves, someone had to keep us alive long enough for the self to arrive. And somewhere near the beginning of almost every life is a mother, or a mothering presence, who gave more than we could understand at the time. This day is for her. For the mothers who are tired. For the mothers who are grieving. For the mothers who feel unseen. For the mothers who gave more than anyone noticed. For the mothers who still wonder whether they did enough. For the mothers who look backward with guilt and wish they could enter old moments with everything they know now. For the mothers who were not perfect, but kept trying. For the mothers who carried children in their bodies. For the mothers who carried children in their hearts. For the grandmothers, aunts, sisters, adoptive mothers, stepmothers, teachers, neighbors, friends, fathers, and chosen family who performed the sacred work of mothering by making the world safer, warmer, and more possible. For the women who wanted to be mothers and could not. For the mothers who lost children. For the children who lost mothers. For the relationships that are tender. For the relationships that are complicated. For the love that was clear. For the love that was imperfect. For the care that arrived beautifully. For the care that arrived clumsily but still tried to arrive. May this day be large enough to hold all of it. May it not require mothers to become saints in order to be honored. May it not require children to lie in order to be grateful. May it make room for truth, tenderness, grief, repair, memory, and recognition. May it remind us that care is not small because it is ordinary. May it remind us that love is not meaningless because it was imperfect. May it remind us that no one becomes human alone. And if you are a mother reading this who carries guilt, I hope you know this: the fact that you still wonder whether your love was enough may itself be evidence that your love is still alive in you. Still reaching. Still learning. Still grieving. Still trying to bless what it cannot change. You do not have to have done everything perfectly to have loved deeply. Your love did not have to be flawless to matter. And maybe that is one of the most healing things Mother’s Day can offer when we let it become more than sentiment: not the fantasy of perfect mothers and perfectly grateful children, but the recognition of human beings who carried one another through the impossible beginning of life. The ones who stayed. The ones who tried. The ones who made a world. And for my mother, Cherie, whose birthday lives beside Mother’s Day: thank you for being born. Thank you for becoming yourself. Thank you for carrying what you could. Thank you for loving me through your own becoming. Thank you for serving in ways I did not always know how to name. Thank you for remembering the details. Thank you for worrying because love made it hard not to. Thank you for wanting me to find true love. Thank you for filling kitchens, birthdays, conversations, and ordinary days with your presence. Thank you for being human. Thank you for becoming my first world. May you feel, in these words, some portion of the love you have spent your life giving away. May you feel blessed. May you feel encouraged. May you feel loved.
