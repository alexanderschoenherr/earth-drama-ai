Trump Is the Government That Would Kill Jesus When a president invokes God while persecuting the stranger, the poor, and the prisoner, Christians have to ask whether they are following Christ — or Rome. Jesus was not killed by immigrants. He was not killed by atheists, feminists, drag queens, socialists, refugees, prisoners, or the poor. Jesus was killed by state power. He was executed by empire — legally, publicly, brutally — because his presence exposed the violence beneath order, the hypocrisy beneath religion, and the fear beneath authority. That matters now because America is living through a grotesque inversion of Christianity: a government that invokes God while persecuting the very people Jesus identified himself with. The hungry. The stranger. The sick. The imprisoned. The poor. The disposable. These were not side characters in Jesus’ moral imagination. They were the place where he said he could be found. So when a president claims the language of Christianity while using the power of the state against immigrants, prisoners, the poor, the sick, and the vulnerable, the question is not merely whether that president is personally religious. The question is deeper. Can American Christianity still recognize Christ when he does not arrive wrapped in a flag? Can it recognize him when he arrives without papers? Can it recognize him when he is poor, detained, foreign, afraid, sick, hungry, or politically inconvenient? Or has too much of American Christianity made the oldest bargain in the world — trading the living Christ for the protection of empire? The Crucifixion Was State Violence The cross was not an accident. It was not random violence. It was not a misunderstanding. It was not a private dispute that got out of hand. The cross was an instrument of the state. Before it became jewelry, before it became church architecture, before it became a symbol polished by centuries of art and doctrine, the cross was a machine of public terror. It was how empire made an example out of people. It was how Rome turned a human body into a warning sign. This is essential to remember. Jesus was not simply “killed.” He was executed. He was processed through power. Accused. Handed over. Judged. Mocked. Displayed. Punished. Raised up in public as a message to everyone watching. This is what governments do when they want violence to look like order. They create procedure. They create paperwork. They create charges. They create spectacle. They create distance between the person giving the order and the body absorbing its consequences. And then they call it law. The crucifixion was legal. That did not make it righteous. That is why the crucifixion should make us suspicious of every political system that believes legality is the same thing as morality. Rome did not have to think of itself as evil to crucify Jesus. Empire rarely does. Empire usually believes it is preserving peace, protecting order, securing borders, managing threats, disciplining disorder, and defending civilization from chaos. That is how power speaks when it wants cruelty to sound responsible. It says: This is necessary. This is security. This is law and order. This is what must happen to keep the rest of us safe. But the Gospel forces a more dangerous question: Safe from whom? Safe from the poor? Safe from the stranger? Safe from the prisoner? Safe from the prophet? Safe from the one who says the last shall be first? Jesus threatened empire not because he commanded an army, but because he revealed a kingdom that did not need one. He threatened domination by refusing to worship it. He threatened hierarchy by blessing the lowly. He threatened religious hypocrisy by exposing it. He threatened political fear by refusing to be governed by it. And so the government did what governments often do when mercy becomes dangerous. It made an example of him. Not because he was weak. Because he revealed too much. Trump Is Not Just a Man. He Is the Government. This is where the analogy matters. Trump is not merely a private citizen with religious opinions. He is not only a man holding a Bible, selling a Bible, quoting God, gesturing toward Christianity, or claiming divine favor. He is the president. He is the executive face of the American state. That means when we talk about what “the government” is doing to immigrants, protesters, prisoners, poor people, sick people, families, workers, and the vulnerable, we are not speaking abstractly. In this moment, Trump is the government. Not the whole government. Not every court, every agency, every official, every citizen. But he is the head of the executive branch. He is the face, voice, will, mood, and command structure of the federal machinery he leads. So the question is not only, “What does Trump believe about Jesus?” The question is: What does Trump’s government do to the people Jesus told Christians to recognize as himself? Because Rome was not judged by its slogans. It was judged by its crosses. A government reveals itself by what it does to the bodies it has power over. Who does it threaten? Who does it cage? Who does it exile? Who does it mock? Who does it strip of dignity? Who does it make afraid? Who does it call dangerous before it harms them? That is where the analogy becomes spiritually serious. Whoever was responsible for killing Jesus was not merely “bad people.” It was power defending itself. It was government preserving order by sacrificing the vulnerable. It was empire turning a living person into a public warning. And if Jesus says he is present in the stranger, the prisoner, the sick, the hungry, and the poor, then a government that targets those people is not simply taking a policy position. It is acting against the places where Christ said he would be found. That is why Trump’s Christianity deserves scrutiny — not as gossip about his private soul, but as a question about public power. A president can say “God” all day. A president can pose with a Bible. A president can speak the language of blessing. A president can be praised by pastors, defended by religious broadcasters, and treated by his followers as a providential instrument. But Christianity is not measured by how loudly a ruler invokes God. It is measured by whether power bends toward mercy. And when power does not bend toward mercy — when it hardens itself against the stranger, the prisoner, the poor, the sick, the grieving, the foreigner, the powerless — then Christians have to ask an older and more dangerous question: Are we watching a government follow Christ? Or are we watching empire wear his name? The Stranger Is Christ Jesus told Christians where to find him. Not in the palace. Not in the throne room. Not in the emperor’s procession. Not in the flag. Not in the spectacle of national greatness. He said: I was hungry. I was thirsty. I was a stranger. I was naked. I was sick. I was in prison. And then he made the identification terrifyingly direct: Whatever you did to the least of these, you did to me. This is one of the most radical claims in Christianity. Jesus does not merely tell his followers to be kind to vulnerable people. He identifies himself with them. The hungry are not a charity category. The stranger is not a political inconvenience. The prisoner is not a disposable body. The sick are not a burden. The poor are not failed citizens. They are the place where Christ said he would be found. That means immigration is not just a policy debate for Christians. It is a spiritual test. Not because every border policy is automatically evil. Not because nations have no right to laws, systems, or processes. But because the moment a government teaches people to see the stranger as vermin, invader, criminal, disease, threat, infestation, or enemy, it has already begun the work of crucifixion. Before vulnerable people are punished, they are renamed. The stranger becomes illegal. The refugee becomes an invader. The asylum seeker becomes a criminal. The poor become parasites. The prisoner becomes a monster. The sick become a cost. The foreigner becomes a threat. And once the image of God has been stripped from a person, almost anything can be done to them. This is why the Christian response to immigration cannot begin with fear. It has to begin with recognition. Who is this person? Not legally first. Not economically first. Not politically first. Spiritually first. Who is this person before God? A child of God. A bearer of the divine image. A neighbor. A stranger. And according to Jesus, possibly Christ himself standing at the gate. That should make Christians careful. Careful with language. Careful with power. Careful with borders. Careful with policies that turn families into targets. Careful with any leader who trains them to feel righteous while becoming cruel. Because the Gospel does not allow Christians to despise the stranger and still pretend they are merely defending civilization. Jesus was clear. The stranger is not outside the moral circle. The stranger is Christ. Jesus Was a Child at the Border There is another part of the story American Christianity often forgets. Before Jesus was worshiped as Lord, he was carried across borders by frightened parents fleeing state violence. In Matthew’s Gospel, Joseph is warned to take Mary and the child to Egypt because Herod is seeking to destroy him. Jesus’ earliest life is marked by political danger, displacement, and escape. The holy family survives because they flee. The child lives because his parents cross into another land. Before Jesus is the teacher, healer, prophet, and crucified one, he is a threatened child in the arms of parents running from a ruler. That should matter. It should matter to every Christian who speaks of borders as though they are clean moral lines between the deserving and the disposable. It should matter to every politician who invokes Jesus while treating desperate families as an infestation. It should matter to every church that sings about the holy family while looking away from families who flee violence, hunger, poverty, and collapse. Because in the Gospel story, Jesus does not enter history as a citizen protected by a stable nation. He enters history as a child whose life is threatened by political power. A child whose parents must move. A child whose safety depends on escape. A child who survives because another land becomes refuge. Before Christians turned Jesus into a border wall, the Gospel made him a refugee child. So when a government teaches Christians to harden their hearts against families at the border, it is not asking them to forget politics. It is asking them to forget part of Jesus. Churches on the Map of Fear This is where the American contradiction becomes unbearable. A president can invoke Christianity while his government expands fear into immigrant communities. A president can hold up a Bible while families wonder whether school, work, court, hospital, or church is safe. A president can speak of God while the machinery of the state treats desperate people as enemies to be hunted, detained, removed, and forgotten. Under Trump, even places once treated with special care — churches, schools, hospitals — have been pulled closer to the machinery of immigration enforcement. The message is clear enough: There may be no sanctuary from the state. That is not just a legal shift. It is a spiritual image. Churches themselves have been pulled into the map of fear. The stranger is not only at the border. The stranger is in the pews. The stranger is in the emergency room. The stranger is dropping a child off at school. The stranger is trying to stand before God while wondering whether the state is waiting outside. But Matthew 25 does not ask whether the ruler used religious language. It asks what happened to the hungry. What happened to the stranger. What happened to the sick. What happened to the prisoner. It asks whether anyone recognized Christ when he arrived without status. This is the test American Christianity keeps trying to avoid. It wants Jesus in the abstract, but not the stranger in the flesh. It wants the cross as symbol, but not the crucified body as neighbor. It wants salvation without recognition. But Jesus did not say, “I was a citizen and you protected me.” He did not say, “I was powerful and you praised me.” He did not say, “I was respectable and you welcomed me.” He said: I was a stranger. And you either welcomed me — or you did not. The Bible as Prop, the Bible as Witness There is a difference between using the Bible and being judged by it. This difference matters now. Because one of the strangest features of American politics is how easily Scripture can be turned into scenery. A Bible can be held up, branded, sold, quoted, waved around, wrapped in patriotic language, and used as proof that the person touching it stands with God. But the Bible is not a loyalty badge. It is not campaign merchandise. It is not a stage prop. It is not a permission slip for domination. It is not a flag with thinner pages. The Bible is a witness. And if Christians believe it is holy, then they should also believe it is dangerous — not only to sinners, foreigners, liberals, criminals, or enemies, but to rulers. Especially to rulers. The Bible does not exist merely to comfort power. Again and again, it judges power. It speaks against Pharaoh. It speaks against Babylon. It speaks against empire. It speaks against false prophets who bless violence and call it peace. It speaks against religious leaders who love public honor while neglecting justice, mercy, and faithfulness. So the question is not whether a president can hold a Bible. The question is whether he can survive being read by one. Trump understands the symbolic power of Christianity. He has posed with a Bible. He has promoted a patriotic Bible wrapped together with American civic texts. He knows what the image does. But the Bible is not proven true when powerful men hold it. The Bible is proven true when it exposes them. That artifact is almost too perfect. Scripture wrapped in nationalism. Christ packaged with country. Holiness sold as identity merchandise. The Bible transformed from a witness against empire into an accessory for empire. But the Bible is not supposed to make empire feel holy. It is supposed to reveal where empire has become cruel. This is why the Bible-as-prop is so spiritually dangerous. It allows a ruler to borrow the aura of Christianity without submitting to the demands of Christ. It allows a political movement to speak the language of faith while evading the judgment of faith. It allows people to feel religious while becoming less merciful. That is the real scandal. Not that Trump uses Christian symbols. Politicians have always used religious symbols. The scandal is that so many Christians accept the symbol as a substitute for the fruit. They see the Bible in the ruler’s hand and stop asking what is happening to the stranger at the gate. They hear the word “God” and stop asking what is happening to the prisoner in the cell. They see pastors lay hands on power and stop asking whether power has laid hands on the poor. But Jesus never told his followers to judge rulers by their branding. He told them to judge by fruit. And the fruit of a politics that invokes God while hardening the heart against the vulnerable is not Christianity. It is empire wearing Christianity as a costume. A Bible in the hand does not sanctify cruelty. A prayer on the stage does not redeem persecution. A cross around the neck does not make domination holy. The Bible is not proven true when powerful men quote it. The Bible is proven true when it exposes them. Why Christians Choose Empire The question is not only why Trump uses Christianity. The harder question is why so many Christians accept the exchange. The support has not been marginal. White evangelical support for Trump has been one of the most durable religious-political alliances in modern American life. That support cannot be explained by Trump’s personal resemblance to Jesus. It has to be explained by something else. Fear. Power. Tribe. Courts. Abortion. Immigration. Resentment. Cultural displacement. Distrust of secular elites. The belief that Christianity is under siege. The belief that a ruthless man may be necessary to protect a righteous cause. This is the bargain. Many Christians do not support Trump because they believe he is gentle, merciful, humble, honest, self-sacrificing, or Christlike. They support him because they believe he is useful. Useful against liberals. Useful against immigrants. Useful against secularism. Useful against feminism. Useful against the courts. Useful against the culture they feel is humiliating them. Useful against a future in which they no longer feel like the natural owners of America. And this is where the spiritual danger begins. Because fear can make domination feel like protection. Fear can make cruelty feel like courage. Fear can make vengeance feel like justice. Fear can make a strongman feel like a savior. Fear can make people betray Jesus while believing they are defending him. This is not new. The temptation has always been to choose the messiah who crushes your enemies over the one who tells you to love them. The temptation has always been to want a kingdom without a cross. A victory without mercy. A savior without surrender. A God who blesses your tribe and punishes everyone who frightens it. That is not Christianity. That is empire with worship music. To be fair, not every Christian who supports Trump is motivated by cruelty. Some are motivated by abortion. Some by religious liberty. Some by economic pain. Some by distrust of institutions. Some by anger at Democratic arrogance. Some by fear that their values are being mocked, erased, or legally cornered. Those fears may explain the bargain. They do not sanctify it. Because Christianity does not teach that fear gives you permission to stop recognizing the image of God in your neighbor. It does not teach that political usefulness cancels moral fruit. It does not teach that a leader can persecute the stranger, mock the weak, threaten enemies, feed contempt, and then be baptized by the fact that he appoints the judges you want. This is where American Christianity has to face itself. Has it chosen Christ? Or has it chosen a protector? Has it chosen the poor man from Nazareth? Or has it chosen the ruler who promises to punish the people it fears? Because there is a difference between being persecuted and being prevented from dominating others. There is a difference between losing cultural privilege and being crucified. There is a difference between following Jesus and demanding that the empire enforce your theology. That distinction matters. A Christianity that needs state power to make others afraid has already lost confidence in the power of Christ. A Christianity that calls cruelty “strength” has forgotten the Sermon on the Mount. A Christianity that sees the stranger as an invader before it sees him as a neighbor has forgotten Matthew 25. A Christianity that excuses every sin of the ruler because he fights its enemies has not found a champion. It has found an idol. And idols always demand sacrifice. In this case, the sacrifice is mercy. The sacrifice is truth. The sacrifice is the stranger. The sacrifice is the prisoner. The sacrifice is the poor. The sacrifice is the ability to recognize Jesus anywhere he does not flatter the tribe. That is the real cost of choosing empire. Not merely that it harms the vulnerable. But that it trains Christians to become the kind of people who can watch the vulnerable be harmed and call it faithfulness. The Places Christ Said He Would Be Christians do not have to guess where Jesus is. He told them. He did not say he would be found only in sanctuaries, hymns, rituals, campaigns, sermons, flags, or Supreme Court victories. He said he would be found in the places human beings are most tempted to look away from. The hungry. The thirsty. The stranger. The naked. The sick. The imprisoned. That list is not sentimental. It is a map. It is a map of the people power usually considers inconvenient. The people empire manages, disciplines, ignores, deports, cages, prices out, mocks, blames, or leaves behind. And Jesus says: There. Look there. That is where I am. This is what makes Christianity so dangerous to empire when it is actually believed. Because empire depends on distance. It needs the prisoner to be only a criminal. It needs the migrant to be only illegal. It needs the poor to be only irresponsible. It needs the sick to be only expensive. It needs the hungry to be only unfortunate. It needs the vulnerable to be reduced to problems that power can solve, punish, or erase. But Jesus collapses that distance. He does not let the prisoner remain only a prisoner. He does not let the stranger remain only a stranger. He does not let the hungry remain only the hungry. He says: That was me. This is not charity. This is judgment. It is judgment on every person, church, party, president, and nation that claims God while stepping over the places God said he would be. And this is why the Christian defense of cruelty is so spiritually grotesque. Because cruelty toward the vulnerable is never only cruelty toward “them.” According to Jesus, it is cruelty toward him. When the stranger is hunted, Christ is there. When the prisoner is forgotten, Christ is there. When the poor are blamed for their own suffering, Christ is there. When the sick are treated as disposable, Christ is there. When children are terrified by the machinery of the state, Christ is there. When families are separated, Christ is there. When people are made afraid to seek help, worship, medical care, or shelter, Christ is there. This is the part American Christianity does not want to face. It wants Jesus as savior, but not Jesus as immigrant. Jesus as king, but not Jesus as prisoner. Jesus as Lord, but not Jesus as hungry child. Jesus as redeemer, but not Jesus as poor mother. Jesus as symbol, but not Jesus as body. But the Gospel does not offer that separation. It does not allow Christians to worship Christ in heaven while despising him at the border. It does not allow them to praise Christ on Sunday while applauding policies that make the stranger afraid on Monday. It does not allow them to sing about mercy while electing cruelty and calling it strength. The test was never whether Christians could recognize Jesus when he looked holy. The test was whether they could recognize him when he looked inconvenient. When he looked foreign. When he looked poor. When he looked imprisoned. When he looked sick. When he looked like someone their politics had trained them to fear. That is the terrifying genius of Matthew 25. Jesus hides himself in the people power teaches us to despise. And then he waits to see what we do. Not what we say. Not what we post. Not what we claim to believe. Not how loudly we defend the Bible. Not how fiercely we insist this is a Christian nation. What we do. Did we feed him? Did we welcome him? Did we visit him? Did we clothe him? Did we care for him? Did we recognize him? Or did we call him a threat? Did we call him illegal? Did we call him criminal? Did we call him weak? Did we call him someone else’s problem? Did we call him the cost of keeping ourselves safe? This is the judgment hidden inside the Gospel: Every empire eventually meets Christ in the body it thought it could discard. And every Christianity that serves empire eventually has to explain why it did not recognize him. The Final Question The point is not that America is Rome in every detail. The point is that empire has a recognizable spirit. It knows how to make violence sound like order. It knows how to make fear sound like wisdom. It knows how to make cruelty sound like strength. It knows how to make law sound like righteousness. It knows how to make religion useful. And the point is not that Trump is Pontius Pilate in a costume. The point is that Trump, as president, holds the power of the state — and when that power is used against the stranger, the prisoner, the poor, the sick, and the vulnerable while wrapped in Christian language, Christians have to ask what story they are actually living inside. Are they standing with Christ? Or are they standing with the machinery that crucifies him? Because the crucifixion was never only about one government in one ancient province doing one terrible thing one time. It was the exposure of a pattern. Power becomes afraid. Religion becomes useful. The crowd becomes angry. The vulnerable body becomes the sacrifice. And everyone involved finds a way to call it necessary. That pattern did not end. It learned new symbols. It learned flags. It learned slogans. It learned cable news. It learned border language. It learned campaign rallies. It learned how to say “God bless America” while making some of God’s children afraid to exist inside it. And this is where American Christianity faces its real test. Not whether it can defend itself against secular culture. Not whether it can win elections. Not whether it can place its language in the law. Not whether it can get close enough to power to feel protected. The test is whether it can still recognize Jesus when Jesus arrives without power. Without papers. Without money. Without status. Without a country willing to receive him. Without the right accent. Without the right politics. Without the right usefulness to the tribe. If Jesus came back as a migrant child, would American Christianity recognize him? If he came back as a detained father, would it recognize him? If he came back as a sick woman afraid to go to the hospital, would it recognize him? If he came back as a prisoner, would it recognize him? If he came back as a poor family at the edge of eviction, would it recognize him? If he came back as a stranger standing outside the gate, asking not for domination but for mercy, would it recognize him? Or would it ask for his papers? Would it call him illegal? Would it say he should have come the right way? Would it accuse him of threatening the country? Would it watch the state take him away and call that law? This is not a call to abandon Christianity. It is a call to stop abandoning Christ. Stop mistaking empire for protection. Stop mistaking cruelty for strength. Stop mistaking fear for discernment. Stop mistaking domination for faith. Stop mistaking a ruler’s religious branding for the fruit of the Spirit. The stranger is still at the gate. The prisoner is still in the cell. The hungry are still waiting. The sick are still afraid. The poor are still being blamed. The vulnerable are still being sacrificed to make the powerful feel safe. And Christ is still asking the same question: Did you recognize me? Because if Jesus came back without papers, too much of American Christianity would not worship him. It would report him.
